A B S T R A C T 

Coefficient inversion on fine grids under PDE constraints is ill conditioned: sparse observations weakly constrain fine-scale parameters, and direct single-resolution optimization must recover state and coefficient fields across all scales simultaneously. This causes slow, initialization-sensitive convergence; learned transfer models require offline data and can introduce approxi-mation error into the numerical physics. We propose PhyRes-MDNF, a fixed-physics multilevel discrete neural field framework. On each level, a single-level DNF represents the inverse unknowns directly as trainable fields and optimizes the discrete objective. In the full-space Darcy realization, state fields 𝑈 and their shared coefficient field 𝐾 are optimized jointly in one fixed-physics inverse process. Between levels, one zero-initialized PhyRes-GNN jointly performs fixed-stencil prolongation and bounded residual correction to construct an incoming target representation, which a fixed initialization map converts to the next DNF variables. It is fitted anew from the observations and unchanged numerical model, without offline pretraining or fine-grid truth. Coarse levels therefore resolve large-scale structure before refined degrees of freedom are introduced, shortening the fine-grid optimization path while retaining the original discrete operator. Under the same final-grid update budget, the multilevel Darcy realization reduces coefficient and state errors by approximately 85% and 90% , respectively, demonstrating improved accuracy and final-grid iteration efficiency. On measured KTC2023 EIT data, the full-

𝑊 pipeline improves mean Otsu mIoU by approximately 3.4% over the official linearized CEM reconstruction and 16 .9% over direct single-level DNF. 

## 1. Introduction 

Coefficient inversion for elliptic PDEs seeks a spatially varying material field from indirect measurements of states, voltages, or fluxes. The problem is intrinsically ill conditioned: observations are sparse relative to the number of coefficient degrees of freedom, fine-scale modes are only weakly visible, and errors in the reconstructed state can be absorbed by the coefficient. On a fine grid, direct single-resolution optimization must recover large- and small-scale structures simultaneously. This produces a long, initialization-sensitive optimization path and can cause the coefficient and state variables to settle into an inaccurate local solution before the large-scale structure has been identified [13, 30, 31]. Classical finite-difference and finite-element inverse methods retain a trusted discrete forward model, but opti-mizing all fine-grid inverse variables from the start remains expensive and poorly conditioned. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) instead represent states and coefficients with coordinate networks and fit their weights from observation and residual losses [16, 25]. Although flexible, this representation can introduce spectral bias, loss imbalance, and a mismatch between continuous automatic differentiation and the discrete operator used to interpret the measurements [32]. Learned surrogates and neural operators offer fast repeated inference, but typically require an offline dataset spanning coefficients, sources, and observations [18, 20]. Moreover, surrogate error is especially risky in coefficient inversion because it can be misinterpreted as a physically plausible change in the unknown coefficient. These limitations motivate a method that retains the prescribed numerical operator, avoids dataset pretraining, and reduces the difficult fine-grid optimization burden.

We propose PhyRes-MDNF , a fixed-physics multilevel discrete neural field framework. A discrete neural field (DNF) represents the inverse unknowns themselves as trainable grid or mesh arrays rather than as the outputs of a coordinate network. On one level, the arrays are optimized by automatic differentiation through a prescribed finite-dimensional objective. In the full-space Darcy realization, the pressure fields 𝑈 𝑚 for all sources and their shared permeability field 𝐾 are placed in the same objective and optimized jointly. State and coefficient recovery therefore exchange information within one inverse optimization process. In the reduced KTC2023 EIT realization, the electrical states are eliminated by the linearized complete electrode model (CEM), and the trainable DNF variable is the conductivity contrast sampled to the official finite-element mesh. Repeating the single-level DNF solve on nested discretizations gives the multilevel DNF (MDNF) backbone. Coarse levels identify strongly observable large-scale structure before weakly constrained refined degrees of freedom are introduced. This continuation does not remove the cost of the coarse solves, but it shortens the effective fine-grid optimization path and improves iteration efficiency at the expensive final resolution. The idealized equal-step analysis in Section 3 makes this mechanism explicit: coarse optimization can reduce slowly decaying error modes before the direct and multilevel paths receive the same number of final-grid updates. This hierarchy is connected to classical subspace-correction and cascadic multigrid ideas, which use nested spaces to remove error components at the resolutions where they are most efficiently represented [3, 34]. Multigrid strategies have also been developed for discretized optimization and distributed parameter estimation [2, 5, 22], while recent multilevel optimization theory addresses inverse problems more broadly [33]. PhyRes-MDNF differs in combining this continuation principle with direct trainable discrete fields and a per-instance learned transfer correction. The second component is a physics-coupled GNN transfer (PhyRes-GNN) used at each coarse-to-fine interface. At an interface, the GNN maps the coarse fields to an incoming target representation in one forward transfer, and a fixed initialization map converts that representation into the next DNF variables. Its graph layer uses fixed coarse-to-fine neighbor weights and its zero-initialized trainable branch supplies a bounded residual, so prolongation and correction are jointly realized inside the single mapping 𝑇 𝓁 →𝓁 +1

2.4. Neural-network approaches to inverse problems 

Besides conventional numerical inversion, neural networks can be used to parameterize the unknown states, coefficients, or the map from observations to reconstructions. Some neural inverse models are trained offline from paired samples and then evaluated on new data. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) provide a different, per-instance approach: they optimize a new set of network parameters for the current observations and governing equations, and therefore require neither paired training data nor offline pretraining [16, 25]. In an inverse PINN, coordinate networks represent the states and coefficient, 

𝑢 𝑚 (𝑥 ) ≈ 𝑢 𝜃 𝑚 (𝑥 ), 𝜅 (𝑥 ) ≈ 𝜅 𝜓 (𝑥 ),

and automatic differentiation evaluates the continuous residuals. A generic training objective is 

PINN = 𝜆 data data + 𝜆 pde pde + 𝜆 bc bc + 𝜆 reg (𝜅 𝜓 ). (22) The state-network parameters {𝜃 𝑚 } and the shared coefficient parameters 𝜓 are fitted jointly from measurements and collocation residuals. Thus PINNs avoid a problem-specific discrete adjoint and can be applied directly to a single inverse instance. Their performance, however, depends on the coordinate-network parameterization, collocation rule, and balance among the data, PDE, boundary, and regularization terms. For ill-conditioned coefficient inversion, the resulting nonconvex optimization can exhibit spectral bias, slow convergence, and competition between state and coefficient errors [32]. Per-instance physics-informed inversion has been studied in subsurface flow and transport, including hydraulic tomography, multiphysics data assimilation, and inverse differential-equation formulations [9, 10, 17]. These examples motivate physics-based learning without paired solution data, but they retain a neural parameterization of the unknown fields. A different line of work makes discrete numerical quantities differentiable and trainable. Physics-informed convolutional models evaluate prescribed discrete operators on grids [35], PIXEL uses trainable cell representations for PDE solutions [14], and ODIL directly optimizes a discrete physical loss without a neural solution network [15]. Conservative PINNs on discrete subdomains provide a related discretization-aware treatment of conservation laws [12]. These works support the distinction between a trainable discrete field and a learned surrogate of the numerical operator. Graph neural networks provide a complementary representation for information exchange on grids and meshes. Message-passing neural networks define learned node updates from graph-local aggregations [8], and MeshGraphNets apply this principle to mesh-based simulation [23]. GNNs have also been used to learn algebraic multigrid prolongation and multilevel domain-decomposition components [21, 29]. In the proposed method, by contrast, graph aggregation is restricted to the transfer interface and the physical operator used in the inverse objective remains fixed. The numerical and PINN approaches therefore have complementary limitations. Classical numerical methods retain a trusted discretization and accurately enforce the selected forward model, but they commonly require problem-dependent forward solvers, sensitivities, or adjoint implementations; direct fine-grid inversion can also be expensive and poorly conditioned. PINNs remove the need for an offline dataset and explicit discrete adjoint, but replace the discrete unknowns by coordinate-network weights and require a carefully balanced residual optimization. In both approaches, activating all high-resolution degrees of freedom from the beginning makes weakly observable fine-scale coefficient modes difficult to recover and can lead to slow, initialization-sensitive optimization. 

## 3. PhyRes-MDNF: Single-Level DNF Optimization and Physics-Coupled Residual GNN Correction 

Section 2 contrasted classical numerical inversion, which retains a prescribed discrete forward model, with per-instance PINN optimization, which requires no offline training data. We now combine the relevant advantages through three design choices. First, the inverse variables are stored directly as trainable grid or mesh fields and optimized under a prespecified discrete level objective; we call this fixed-level operation a discrete neural field (DNF) solve. Second, the DNF solve is repeated on a nested coarse-to-fine sequence, giving a multilevel discrete neural field (MDNF) backbone. Third, a single objective-only transfer operator jointly performs fixed-stencil prolongation and bounded residual correction without fine-grid truth. We call this transfer module the physics-coupled residual GNN (PhyRes-GNN).   

> Lu et al.: Preprint submitted to Elsevier Page 6 of 22 PhyRes-MDNF for Inverse Problems

These three design choices define the complete PhyRes-MDNF framework. Each coarse-to-fine transition has two components. A single-level DNF solve optimizes the active inverse variables, and PhyRes-GNN constructs a prolonged-and-corrected incoming representation. The fixed initialization map then converts it into the variables for the next DNF solve. The finite-difference, finite-element, or CEM operator remains fixed in both components; the learned corrector neither replaces the numerical physics nor defines the final reconstruction objective. The remainder of this section defines this algorithmic path at the framework level. The concrete Darcy residual, KTC2023 EIT sensitivity matrix, and problem-specific features are deferred to Section 4.

4.5. Summary of the two realizations 

Both realizations follow PhyRes-MDNF: a single-level DNF solve is passed through one PhyRes-GNN transfer operator, which jointly prolongs and corrects an incoming representation before  initializes the refined DNF solve. In EIT, this representation is the auxiliary 64 2 grid and the refined DNF variable is the FEM-mesh contrast. The two realizations differ in their active fields, incoming transfer representations, and objective terms. Darcy uses a full-space objective that jointly updates pressure states and permeability. KTC2023 EIT jointly minimizes its voltage-consistency and conductivity blocks through a contrast field under the linearized CEM. In both cases, the GNN is restricted to the transfer interface, and the final inverse solution is produced by the fixed problem-specific objective.

## 5. Numerical Experiments 

We evaluate the two components of PhyRes-MDNF separately and together. We first use a basic Darcy inverse problem to show that repeated single-level DNF optimization along a coarse-to-fine path attains higher final accuracy after the same number of final-grid updates. We then isolate the inter-level transfer on LUNDIsim and compare deterministic interpolation with PhyRes-GNN correction, followed by a longer two-dimensional multilevel realization. The section closes with KTC2023 EIT as a cross-physics test of framework-level generalizability, where the official complete electrode model linearized reconstruction provides a strong discrete inverse baseline. Throughout all experiments, the discrete physics operator is fixed. The learned component does not replace the finite-difference, finite-element, or complete electrode model operator. It only changes the initial point passed to the next refined inverse optimization. 

5.1. Experimental setup 

The Darcy experiments use a conservative finite-difference residual. The unknowns are the state field 𝑈 and the permeability coefficient 𝐾 , and both are represented directly as trainable discrete fields. The basic Darcy case uses a manufactured coefficient so that the role of the multilevel path can be observed without distribution shift. LUNDIsim replaces the manufactured coefficient by reservoir-like fields [7]. The hierarchy studies optimize only the stated MDNF objective. The Darcy transfer correctors are likewise fitted without access to fine-grid 𝑈 ⋆ or 𝐾 ⋆: their loss contains only the refined PDE residual and observed pressure mismatch. A new corrector is fitted for each target, so these are objective-only per-instance transfer ablations and do not test a corrector pretrained on separate LUNDIsim fields. We use 𝑤 pde = 1 , 𝑤 obs = 1000 , and 𝜖 𝑢 = 𝜖 𝐾 = 0 .1; the correctors are fitted for 3000 Adam steps. The KTC2023 EIT experiments use the official complete electrode model finite-element Jacobian and the official active voltage mask for category 1. For each of the four phantoms, we optimize the linearized difference objective defined in (52). We report the relative voltage residual relV = ‖𝐽 𝛿𝜎 − Δ 𝑈 ‖2∕‖Δ𝑈 ‖2 and the official two-threshold Otsu mIoU over background, resistive, and conductive classes. Since Otsu thresholding is a postprocessing step, we also use KTC2023 to inspect whether the refined continuous field and the final segmentation tell the same story. For every phantom, the transfer corrector is reinitialized and trained only through the fixed linearized objective. No conductivity truth is used for that training, and no learned weights are shared between phantoms or with the Darcy experiments. The coordinate-network PINN baseline uses all 76 current-injection patterns, a four-layer width-128 network, 10000 Adam updates, and GPU training. Its conductivity and state networks are fitted from the PDE, CEM boundary, current, and measured-voltage losses without the official FEM Jacobian. The same three-class IoU averaging convention is used for evaluation. On Data 3 and Data 4, however, the recovered field does not admit a valid two-threshold Otsu split. Those entries, and consequently the aggregate PINN mean, are reported as N/A rather than mixing a binary fallback with the required three-class metric. The direct single-level baseline starts from zero on one 64 2 reconstruction grid and performs 10000 Adam updates with the full measurement matrix 𝑊 and the conductivity-regularization setting used by the official reconstruction. It has no coarse solve, inter-level transfer, PhyRes-GNN, or mesh unlock. The complete PhyRes-MDNF pipeline has a 32 2 coarse-grid DNF level and a 1602-node FEM-mesh DNF level. It first performs 1200 Adam updates on the coarse grid. One PhyRes-GNN transfer, fitted with eight LBFGS iterations at learning rate 0.3, then constructs the incoming representation on an auxiliary 64 2 grid; the fixed initialization map samples this corrected field to the FEM mesh. There is no intervening 64 2 DNF solve. Finally, the mesh DNF performs 60 outer LBFGS updates with full 

𝑊 . Thus the single-level row tests direct one-level DNF optimization, but it is not an equal-optimizer or equal-total-cost ablation of the complete pipeline. The causal transfer ablation is instead provided by the matched LUNDIsim experiment in Section 5.3; the KTC2023 experiment compares complete reconstruction pipelines across numerical, coordinate-network, single-level discrete-field, and PhyRes-MDNF approaches.

5.3. Transfer ablation: objective-only residual correction improves the warm start 

After the multilevel backbone is fixed, we isolate the transfer from a coarse solve to a refined solve. Table 2 compares two 64 2 → 128 2 LUNDIsim transfers with the high-level coefficient unlocked. The residual corrector is fitted to this target instance using the physics-and-observation warm-start loss in (44); the fine-grid reference fields are withheld until evaluation. Bilinear interpolation gives a valid geometric transfer, but it leaves a large initial residual on the refined grid. The objective-only PhyRes-GNN lowers the normalized PDE loss from 3.6991 × 10 −1 to 1.4137 × 10 −1 , a 2.62× 

reduction. The observation loss falls from 6.2403 × 10 −3 to 1.6227 × 10 −3 . Residual correction roughly halves the initial state error, but its initial coefficient error increases slightly because 𝐾 ⋆ is not used for training. After the same refined optimization, it improves both final errors over interpolation; the final coefficient error is approximately 7% 

lower. Figure 4 aligns the two branches by the number of target-grid DNF updates after their respective initializations have been constructed. The residual initialization starts with a lower state error but a slightly higher coefficient error. After the first recorded 500 fine-grid steps, however, both PhyRes-GNN curves are below their interpolation counterparts and retain this advantage through 10000 steps. At the common endpoint, Table 2 gives state errors of 1.6644 × 10 −4 

versus 1.8092 × 10 −4 and coefficient errors of 1.3982 × 10 −2 versus 1.5031 × 10 −2 . Thus the learned correction yields a better refined optimization path under the same number of fine-grid updates. The 3000 correction-training steps occur before the axis in Figure 4; consequently, this figure is a fixed-fine-step comparison, not an equal-total-cost claim. This ablation clarifies the role of the GNN. It is not a learned PDE solver and it is not the final inverse objective. It fits the bounded residual component of the unified GNN transfer, and the fixed-operator MDNF solve then decides whether that initialization is physically useful.

5.5. KTC2023 EIT: framework-level generalization across physics 

KTC2023 changes both the inverse problem and the discretization: the forward model is the complete electrode model, and the official baseline is a linearized finite-element reconstruction. Table 3 compares the official linearized CEM reconstruction, the evaluated coordinate-network PINN, direct single-level DNF, and the complete PhyRes-MDNF pipeline containing PhyRes-GNN transfer and mesh-unlocked refinement. The complete pipeline uses the full 𝑊 

matrix and the measurement-energy rule in (56); no phantom-specific manual selection of 𝜆 𝐾 is performed. The transfer corrector uses eight LBFGS iterations with learning rate (0.3) for every phantom. The complete pipeline reaches 0.623

Figure 6 shows the final Otsu segmentations underlying the official and complete-pipeline entries in Table 3. The complete PhyRes-MDNF reconstruction retains the correct anomaly classes in all four phantoms and improves the three-class mIoU in every case. The gains on Data 3 and Data 4 are modest, consistent with the sensitivity of their sharp square and cross supports to thresholding and boundary smoothing. The direct single-level DNF reaches 0.533 mean mIoU, compared with 0.623 for the complete multilevel pipeline. This gap remains even though the single-level baseline receives a deliberately generous 10000-step Adam budget, far more nominal optimizer updates than the 1200 coarse-grid Adam updates, eight corrector LBFGS iterations, and 60 outer mesh-LBFGS updates in the complete pipeline. Thus direct one-level optimization does not catch up with the multilevel construction under this enlarged single-level budget. This is still not a strict equal-cost or wall-clock claim because an Adam update and an LBFGS outer update have different numbers and costs of objective evaluations. The evaluated PINN reaches three-class mIoU values of 0.201 and 0.159 on Data 1 and Data 2, but does not produce a valid three-class Otsu split on Data 3 or Data 4; an aggregate PINN mean is therefore not reported. Relative to the official method, the complete pipeline improves all four cases, with the largest absolute gain occurring on Data 1. This comparison evaluates the complete KTC2023 pipeline; the matched LUNDIsim experiment in Section 5.3 isolates the causal effect of the PhyRes-GNN transfer. The official and DNF results use the official two-threshold Otsu protocol; as noted above, the PINN fails this three-class segmentation on Data 3 and Data 4. Ground-truth labels are used only for evaluation, not for computing 𝜆 𝐾 or fitting the corrector.

The experiments provide three central comparisons. First, on a matched 128 2 final grid, the multilevel Darcy path attains lower coefficient and state errors than direct single-level optimization by factors of 6.76× and 10 .4× ,respectively. Second, on the two-dimensional LUNDIsim transfer, the objective-only PhyRes-GNN produces a more PDE-consistent warm start, reducing the initial PDE loss by 2.62× , and lowers the final coefficient error by approximately 7% relative to simple interpolation. This third comparison evaluates the complete pipeline rather than isolating the causal contribution of PhyRes-GNN alone. ## 6. Conclusion 

This paper presented PhyRes-MDNF for PDE inverse problems. The complete framework contains two compo-nents: single-level DNF optimization, repeated along a coarse-to-fine hierarchy, and a physics-coupled residual GNN (PhyRes-GNN) transfer that jointly prolongs and corrects between levels. The central design choice is to keep the problem-specific numerical operator fixed while prescribing separate reconstruction and transfer-stage objectives: the coarse-to-fine hierarchy is used as the main stabilization mechanism, and the GNN is used only at the transfer interface. One PhyRes-GNN operator maps a coarse solution to a prolonged-and-corrected incoming representation; a fixed initialization map converts it to the refined DNF variables, after which the normal single-level DNF optimizer resumes. The experiments support this decomposition. At the same manufactured Darcy 128 2 final grid, direct single-level optimization ends at a permeability error of 5.4519 × 10 −3 , whereas the 64 2 → 128 2 path reaches 8.0597 × 10 −4 ;the coefficient and state errors are respectively 6.76× and 10 .4× lower. This is a final-accuracy comparison rather than an equal-cost claim because the multilevel path also performs the coarse solve. The tuned LUNDIsim 16 2 →

32 2 → 64 2 → 128 2 hierarchy reaches 1.3646 × 10 −2 , demonstrating the applicability of the longer multilevel path to a sharper geological field. In a separate matched 64 2 → 128 2 transfer ablation, PhyRes-GNN improves the refined initialization and the subsequent optimization path. In these Darcy tests, a separate corrector is trained for each target using only the refined PDE residual and observed pressures; reference fine-grid fields are reserved for evaluation. The results quantify per-instance objective fitting rather than cross-instance weight generalization. On two-dimensional LUNDIsim Darcy inversion, residual correction reduces the refined initial PDE loss by 2.62× and, with high-level 𝐾 

unlocked, improves the final permeability error from 1.5031 × 10 −2 to 1.3982 × 10 −2 , a reduction of approximately 7% .On KTC2023 EIT, which changes both the PDE and measurement model, a new corrector is fitted for each phantom without conductivity truth. The PhyRes-MDNF pipeline with mesh-unlocked refinement exceeds the official linearized CEM baseline in mean Otsu IoU, reaching 0.623 compared with 0.603 , versus 0.533 for direct single-level DNF. The evaluated coordinate-network PINN yields valid three-class scores only on Data 1 and Data 2, so no aggregate PINN mean is reported. Together, the LUNDIsim Darcy and measured KTC2023 EIT results demonstrate framework-level generalizability across coefficient structures, governing physics, discretizations, and observation geometries. This claim concerns re-instantiation of the same algorithmic framework, not reuse of one trained corrector. The cross-problem result demonstrates localization and support recovery on measured data with complex anomaly geometries; it does not isolate the residual corrector from the subsequent mesh-level refinement or establish exact recovery of sharp corners. The same experiments also show the limits. On LUNDIsim, the large gain in initial residual consistency becomes a smaller coefficient-error gain after both initializations receive the same refined optimization. In KTC2023 EIT, the official linearized Tikhonov reconstruction remains close on several per-phantom IoU values, and the final ranking remains sensitive to thresholding of the continuous conductivity perturbation. Thus the current PhyRes-GNN should be viewed as the residual-correction component of PhyRes-MDNF, rather than a standalone reconstruction model or a learned replacement for the numerical operator. Future work should make the transfer cost model more explicit, comparing wall-clock time rather than optimizer steps across levels. For Darcy, the next step is to complement the present objective-only per-instance corrector with a model trained on separate geologies and evaluated on held-out fields and source configurations. For EIT, the most immediate improvement is to combine the continuous voltage objective with threshold-sensitivity or segmentation-aware losses, so that reductions in data residual translate more reliably into the classwise metrics used for KTC scoring.

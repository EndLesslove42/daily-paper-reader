Abstract 

Reliable nowcasting of PM 2.5 is of practical importance for daily air-quality monitoring and urban management. PM 2.5 concentrations are jointly influenced by emission sources, meteorological conditions, temporal patterns, and station heterogeneity, and the explanatory variables exhibit strong correlations. This study compares regularized regression methods (Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net) with deep learning models (MLP and LSTM) for same-hour PM 2.5 estimation using hourly observations from 12 monitoring stations in Beijing from March 2013 to February 2017. Because the feature set includes both PM 2.5 lagged terms and synchronous co-pollutant measurements, the task is framed as nowcasting rather than strict forecasting. Model evaluation employs timestamp-based chronological train/test splitting and TimeSeriesSplit cross-validation. The MLP achieves the best performance (RMSE = 13.651 μg/m 3, R2 = 0 .972), reducing RMSE by approximately 13% relative to all regularized regression models (RMSE ≈ 15.6, R2 ≈ 0.964). The three linear models perform nearly identically. As an exploratory supplement, the LSTM—constrained by CPU computational limitations to a subsampled training set—underperforms (RMSE = 26.553, R2 = 0 .889) and is not treated as a primary conclusion. Feature-group ablation shows that lagged terms and synchronous pollutants carry the dominant estimation information. Variable selection stability analysis under the 1SE rule reveals that Lasso favors sparse selection (4 stable variables) while Elastic Net retains correlated variable groups (11 stable variables); CO, NO 2, PM 10 , and the first-order PM 2.5 lag form a robust core set. Per-station and monthly error analyses further reveal spatial and seasonal heterogeneity in model errors. 

Keywords: PM 2.5 ; Synchronous covariates; Lagged features; Regularized regression; MLP; Variable selection stability

Fine particulate matter (PM 2.5 ) is a common indicator of urban air pollution, associated with respiratory and cardiovascular health risks and reduced atmospheric visibility. Accurate as-sessment and short-term modeling of PM 2.5 concentrations can support pollution monitoring, traffic management, and environmental policy evaluation. Beijing, with its high population den-sity and intense traffic activity, frequently experiences severe haze episodes during winter heating seasons and under unfavorable meteorological conditions. Severe air-pollution episodes and sub-sequent air-quality improvements in Beijing have been documented in previous environmental studies [10]. From an applied statistics perspective, real-time air-quality estimation can be formulated as a regression problem with temporal dependence, multicollinearity, and station heterogeneity. Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is simple and interpretable, but coefficient estimates become unstable when explanatory variables are highly correlated. This situation is prevalent in air-quality data: PM 2.5 , PM 10 , CO, and NO 2 often reflect related emission sources and dispersion conditions. Regularized regression introduces penalty terms to trade a small amount of bias for variance reduction, alleviating multicollinearity and—for certain penalty forms—performing variable selection [3, 8, 11]. Meanwhile, deep learning models such as the multilayer perceptron (MLP) and long short-term memory network (LSTM) [4] can capture nonlinear interactions and temporal dependencies that linear models may miss. When models serve explanatory purposes, estimation accuracy alone is insufficient. In the presence of correlated explanatory variables, the selected variable set may vary considerably across subsamples, undermining the reliability of variable-level conclusions. Stability selection [6, 7] addresses this by repeatedly refitting the model on subsamples and recording how frequently each variable is selected. Based on the publicly available Beijing Multi-Site Air-Quality Data Set [9], this study addresses three questions: 1. Under a time-aware evaluation scheme, how do regularized regression (Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net) and deep learning (MLP, LSTM) compare in the task of real-time PM 2.5 nowcasting? The LSTM serves as an exploratory supplement; due to computational constraints it was not fully trained and is not treated as a core comparison target. 2. What is the contribution of each feature group (lagged terms, synchronous pollutants, mete-orological variables, temporal variables, station identifiers)? 3. Under the 1SE rule and time-block subsampling, how stable are the selected variables, and is this finding robust across different selection-frequency thresholds? The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the regularized re-gression models, deep learning models, and the stability analysis framework. Section 3 describes the dataset and experimental setup. Section 4 presents the results and analysis. Section 5 2discusses the findings and limitations. Section 6 concludes the paper.

2.4 Time-Series Splitting and Cross-Validation 

All observations are sorted by timestamp before splitting. The first 80% of chronologically ordered observations form the training set, and the remaining 20% form the test set. Within the training set, regularized regression models use TimeSeriesSplit (k = 5) for cross-validation. The regularization parameter grid is λ ∈ { 10 −3, 10 −2.9, . . . , 10 3} (50 log-spaced points), and the Elastic Net L1 ratio grid is α ∈ { 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95 , 1.0}. The StandardScaler is fit only on the training set and then applied to the test set. For deep learning models, the validation set is the last 10% of the training set (strictly chronological, no shuffling), used to monitor overfitting and retain the model weights with the lowest validation loss. Both the MLP and LSTM are trained once (no repeated runs or averaging); the random seed is set to 42 (both 

numpy and torch are fixed). 

2.5 One-Standard-Error Rule and Stability Selection 

The one-standard-error (1SE) rule selects the largest λ satisfying CV( λ) ≤ CV( λmin )+SE( λmin ), yielding a more parsimonious model. Stability selection is performed on contiguous time blocks: starting from the beginning of the training set, contiguous time blocks of fixed window size (approximately 6,000 consecutive observations) are used as subsamples, sliding forward with 50% overlap, forming B = 50 time-block subsamples. For each block subsample, the model is refitted with the 1SE parameter, and whether each variable is selected is recorded. The selection frequency πj of variable j is computed over the B subsamples; variables with πj ≥ π0 (default 0.8) are considered stable. 

# 3 Data and Experimental Setup 

3.1 Dataset 

This study uses the Beijing Multi-Site Air-Quality Data Set [9] from the UCI Machine Learning Repository, containing hourly observations from 12 monitoring stations in Beijing from March 2013 to February 2017 (approximately 420,000 records). Each record includes six pollutant indicators (PM 2.5 , PM 10 , SO 2, NO 2, CO, O 3), five meteorological variables (TEMP, PRES, DEWP, RAIN, WSPM), categorical variables (station, wind direction), and time fields. PM 2.5 

serves as the response variable. 43.2 Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering 

Samples with missing PM 2.5 (2.08%) are removed. Missing values in explanatory variables are imputed with the training-set median (numeric) or mode (categorical). Five feature groups are constructed, yielding 56 features after one-hot encoding: 

• Synchronous pollutants: PM 10 , SO 2, NO 2, CO, O 3.

• Meteorological variables: TEMP, PRES, DEWP, RAIN, WSPM, wind direction (one-hot encoded). 

• Temporal variables: year, month, day, hour, day of week, weekend flag, season (one-hot encoded). 

• Cyclical encoding: sin / cos transformations for hour and month. 

• PM 2.5 lag features: 1-hour, 3-hour, 24-hour lags and a trailing 24-hour rolling mean (shifted by 1 step before taking the window mean, excluding the current-hour PM 2.5 to prevent target leakage), constructed per station. All preprocessing parameters are fit only on the training set and then applied to the test set.

54.2 Model Performance Comparison 

Table 2 and Figure 1 report the test-set performance of all models. The MLP achieves the best performance (RMSE = 13.651, R2 = 0 .972), reducing RMSE by approximately 13% relative to all regularized regression models. The three regularized models perform nearly identically (RMSE ≈ 15.6, R2 ≈ 0.964), consistent with the observation that the current feature set has captured most of the linear estimation information. All models significantly outperform the TrainMean baseline (RMSE = 82.041) and the Lag1Persistence baseline (RMSE = 19.869). As an exploratory supplement, the LSTM underperforms (RMSE = 26.553, R2 = 0 .889), even worse than the Lag1Persistence baseline. This underperformance may reflect the con-strained CPU-only training budget, the use of randomly subsampled sequences, and the noise introduced by global sequence construction across stations. Because the training is insufficient and the experimental setup is not comparable to the other models, the LSTM results serve only as an exploratory reference and are not incorporated into the main conclusions. Figure 2 shows the training and validation loss curves for both deep learning models. The MLP converges smoothly, while the LSTM is still decreasing at epoch 20, suggesting that more training data and epochs may improve its performance.

4.4 Variable Selection Stability 

Under the 1SE rule, the λ1SE values for Lasso and Elastic Net are approximately 19.5 and 8.2 times their respective λmin values. Table 4 summarizes the stability results at π0 = 0 .8. ElasticNet-1SE identifies 11 stable variables (all with πj = 1 .0), while Lasso-1SE identifies only 4 (3 with πj = 1 .0). The four variables stable in both models—CO, NO 2, PM 10 , and PM 2.5 lag1—form the core stable set, with clear physical interpretations: CO and NO 2 are related to combustion emissions, PM 10 shares emission sources with PM 2.5 , and the first-order lag captures short-term autocorrelation.

# 5 Discussion 

The results can be summarized as follows. First, the MLP achieves the best performance, reduc-ing RMSE by approximately 13% relative to all regularized regression models. This indicates that nonlinear interactions among features (which linear models cannot capture) carry addi-tional estimation signal. The gap is not large ( R2: 0.972 vs. 0.964), suggesting that the linear models have already extracted most of the explainable variance, but the MLP’s advantage is consistent and meaningful. Second, the three regularized models perform nearly identically, confirming that when care-fully designed lagged terms and synchronous pollutant features are included, the choice of penalty structure has limited impact on point-estimation accuracy. This comparison remains valuable because the three penalties handle correlated variables differently: Ridge shrinks uniformly, Lasso selects sparsely, and Elastic Net retains variable groups. Third, the LSTM, as an exploratory supplement, underperforms expectations. This result may be related to two practical limitations: (1) CPU computational limitations restricted the training data to 80,000 subsampled sequences out of 290,272 available; (2) global sequence con-struction mixed observations from different stations within a single 24-hour window, introducing noise. It should be emphasized that a single LSTM training run on CPU takes approximately 8– 10 times as long as the MLP, and under constrained computational resources, adequate training is difficult. This exploratory result indicates that sequence models face high computational and time costs in multi-station nowcasting tasks; deployment in a real-time air-quality monitoring system would require GPU resources and station-specific sequence engineering. Because the experimental setup is not comparable, this result should not be interpreted as a rejection of the LSTM method itself, but only as a practical reference. Fourth, the ablation experiment confirms that lagged terms and synchronous pollutants are the most informative feature groups. A compact model using only PM 2.5 lagged terms and a few co-pollutants can approach the accuracy of the full-feature model. 10 Fifth, the stability analysis reveals a consistent difference between Lasso and Elastic Net. The core stable set—CO, NO 2, PM 10 , and PM 2.5 lag1—has reasonable physical interpretations, lending greater credibility to variable-level conclusions. Finally, the spatial and temporal error patterns indicate that aggregate metrics mask sub-group differences. The urban–suburban RMSE gradient and the winter monthly error peak suggest that station-specific or season-specific modeling may yield improvements. This study also has limitations. First, it is restricted to linear regularized models and rel-atively shallow deep learning architectures. Second, because synchronous pollutant variables are observed at the same time as the target, the current setup evaluates same-hour nowcasting rather than strict forecasting. The LSTM results are constrained by CPU computational limi-tations and serve only as an exploratory reference, not as a core conclusion. Future work should explore deeper architectures, GPU-accelerated training, station-specific sequence construction, and a strict forecasting setup. 

# 6 Conclusion 

This study compared regularized regression (Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net) and deep learning (MLP, LSTM) for real-time PM 2.5 nowcasting in Beijing, with a focus on variable selection stability. The main findings are: 1. The MLP achieves the best performance (RMSE = 13.651, R2 = 0 .972), reducing RMSE by approximately 13% relative to regularized regression. The three regularized models perform nearly identically (RMSE ≈ 15.6, R2 ≈ 0.964). 2. As an exploratory supplement, the LSTM is inadequately trained due to computational con-straints (RMSE = 26.553) and takes approximately 8–10 times as long as the MLP on CPU. This result is not an evaluation of the LSTM method itself, but indicates that sequence models face high practical barriers in multi-station nowcasting tasks. 3. Feature-group ablation identifies lagged terms and synchronous pollutants as the most infor-mative feature groups. 4. Lasso-1SE selects 4 stable variables, while ElasticNet-1SE retains 11. CO, NO 2, PM 10 , and PM 2.5 lag1 form the core stable set across all thresholds. 5. Per-station and monthly error analyses reveal spatial and temporal heterogeneity, with higher errors at urban stations and during winter months. Overall, regularized regression provides an interpretable baseline, and the MLP enhances estimation accuracy; together they offer a useful framework for PM 2.5 nowcasting. Future work may extend the framework to deeper architectures, GPU-accelerated training, strict forecasting setups, and richer spatial features. 11 Acknowledgments
